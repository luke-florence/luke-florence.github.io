## Overview

Australian tea tree (*Melaleuca alternifolia*) is the backbone of a multi-million-dollar essential oil industry, yet its belowground biology had never been properly characterised. Most woody plants form an association with just one of two mycorrhizal types — arbuscular mycorrhizal (AM) or ectomycorrhizal (ECM) fungi — each with its own distinct ecological roles. This project combined root anatomy with ITS2 metabarcoding across 40 plants and 5 populations spanning coastal shrublands and upland woodlands in New South Wales to find out which of those partners tea tree actually hosts. The answer: tea tree sits in an unusual third category — the dual mycorrhizal plants — with the rare ability to associate with both AM and ECM fungi and draw on the benefits each group brings.

## Key findings

- **Tea tree is dual mycorrhizal.** 90% of sampled plants simultaneously hosted both arbuscular mycorrhizal (AM) and ectomycorrhizal (ECM) fungi — the first formal demonstration of dual mycorrhizas in *Melaleuca alternifolia*, and a rare trait globally.
- **Coastal and upland ecotypes host distinct fungal communities.** The two habitats partitioned fungal partners strongly, suggesting that local provenance matters when matching plants to soil biota.
- **Native ECM candidates are ready for inoculation trials.** *Tomentella*, *Ruhlandiella*, and *Cenococcum* emerged as dominant, widely distributed partners — strong candidates for nursery-based inoculation to improve seedling establishment and field performance.

## Industry implications

- **Drought and flood resilience.** Dual mycorrhizal strategies hedge against environmental extremes — AM fungi support water and phosphorus uptake under flooding, while ECM fungi support performance in drier, nutrient-limited soils. For a species grown on low-lying coastal floodplains and increasingly exposed to climate volatility, that flexibility is an agronomic asset.
- **Oil yield and plantation performance.** Mycorrhizal status is a lever that tea tree growers haven't yet pulled. Matching plantations with the right native fungal partners may offer a low-cost, biologically grounded intervention for improving seedling vigour, nutrient efficiency, and — ultimately — essential oil yield and quality. That's still speculative at this stage, though, and the logical next step is a coordinated program of pot and field trials to qualify the type of benefit and quantify how much benefit is actually on the table.
