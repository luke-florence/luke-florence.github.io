## Overview

Australian tea tree (*Melaleuca alternifolia*) is the source of tea tree oil and the backbone of an important essential oil industry. Yet one basic part of its biology had not been properly characterised – the fungal partners it associates with belowground.

Most woody plants form close partnerships with mycorrhizal fungi, which help roots capture nutrients and water in exchange for carbon. These partnerships usually fall into one of two major types. **Arbuscular mycorrhizal (AM) fungi** live inside root cells and are often linked to phosphorus uptake and wet or productive environments. **Ectomycorrhizal (ECM) fungi** form sheaths around root tips and are often associated with less productive, drier environments.

Some plants can use both strategies.

This project asked whether tea tree is one of them.

We combined root microscopy with ITS2 DNA metabarcoding across 40 plants from five native populations spanning coastal subtropical forests and upland temperate shrublands in northern New South Wales and southeast Queensland.

![Figure 1](/images/projects/tea-tree-dual-mycorrhizas/figure_1.jpg)

**Figure 1.** Tea tree populations were sampled across contrasting coastal and upland habitats in eastern Australia. Coastal populations occur in warmer, wetter, and more flood-prone environments, while upland populations occur in cooler and drier habitats. This contrast allowed us to test whether tea tree’s fungal partnerships differ across its coastal and upland ecotypes.

The result was clear. Tea tree is a **dual-mycorrhizal plant**. Most sampled individuals hosted both AM and ECM fungi, and their fungal communities differed strongly between coastal and upland habitats.

Our results matter because tea tree already has well-known coastal and upland ecotypes with different shoot and root traits. This study shows that those aboveground and belowground differences extend into the types of fungi it chooses to associate with.

## Key findings

### Tea tree forms both AM and ECM partnerships

Tea tree roots hosted both arbuscular mycorrhizal and ectomycorrhizal fungi.

Microscopy revealed AM structures inside roots, including hyphal coils and vesicles, as well as ECM features such as mantles and superficial Hartig-net hyphae. In most plants, both mycorrhizal types occurred together.

This provides the first formal evidence that *Melaleuca alternifolia* is dual mycorrhizal.

![Figure 2](/images/projects/tea-tree-dual-mycorrhizas/figure_2.png)

**Figure 2.** Tea tree roots showed anatomical evidence of both major mycorrhizal types. Arbuscular mycorrhizal structures occurred inside roots, while ectomycorrhizal features formed around root tips. Together, these observations confirm that *Melaleuca alternifolia* can form both AM and ECM partnerships.

### Coastal and upland ecotypes differed belowground

Tea tree’s fungal partnerships were not the same everywhere.

Upland plants had stronger ECM root colonisation, consistent with their cooler and drier habitats. Coastal plants hosted richer AM and ECM fungal communities, likely reflecting warmer, wetter forests with greater surrounding plant diversity.

This suggests that tea tree’s belowground partnerships are shaped by local habitat conditions.

![Figure 3](/images/projects/tea-tree-dual-mycorrhizas/figure_3.jpeg)

**Figure 3.** Coastal and upland tea tree ecotypes differed in their mycorrhizal associations. Upland plants showed stronger ECM root colonisation, while coastal plants hosted richer AM and ECM fungal communities. These differences suggest that tea tree’s fungal partnerships vary with habitat and provenance.

### Native fungal partners emerged as candidates for future trials

The DNA survey identified several common ECM fungal partners associated with tea tree roots, including **Tomentella**, **Ruhlandiella**, **Sebacina**, and **Cenococcum**.

Some of these fungi are widespread in Australian forests and known to associate with other Myrtaceae. That makes them useful candidates for future inoculation trials testing whether native fungi can improve seedling establishment, stress tolerance, or plantation performance.

At this stage, the study identifies promising partners. It does not prove agronomic benefits. That is the next step.

## Industry implications

Tea tree is grown across landscapes exposed to flooding, drought, nutrient limitation, and increasing climate variability. Dual mycorrhizal flexibility may therefore be an important biological asset.

AM fungi may help tea tree maintain nutrient uptake in wet or waterlogged soils, while ECM fungi may be more important in drier, or nutrient-limited habitats. If their functions can be harnessed, mycorrhizal management could become a practical tool for improving nursery production, seedling establishment, and plantation resilience.

The key opportunity is not simply to add fungi. It is to match the right plant provenance with the right native fungal partners under the right environmental conditions.

Future pot and field trials are needed to test whether these partnerships improve growth, survival, oil yield, or oil quality.

## Why it matters

We show that tea tree belongs to a small but important group of plants capable of forming both AM and ECM partnerships. We provide a baseline for testing whether native fungal partners can support better essential oil quality and yield.
