## Overview

Most trees do not explore the soil alone. They rely on mycorrhizal fungi — belowground partners that help roots capture water and nutrients in exchange for carbon.

But not all partnerships work the same way. Globally, most forest trees use one of two dominant mycorrhizal strategies, and each comes with a unique ecological toolkit.

**Arbuscular mycorrhizal (AM) fungi** are common in warm, wet tropical forests. **Ectomycorrhizal (EcM) fungi** are often linked to cooler, drier, or nutrient-limited forests.

These differences matter. Because AM and EcM trees tend to occupy distinct environmental niches, their fungal partners provide different strategies for accessing nutrients and coping with environmental stress, such as drought and nutrient limitation.

A smaller group of trees can form partnerships with **both AM and EcM fungi**. These are **dual-mycorrhizal trees**, and they may have an unusual advantage — ecological flexibility.

This project asks two simple ecological questions at continental scale:

- **Does a tree’s mycorrhizal strategy help determine where it lives?**
- **Does the dual-mycorrhizal strategy allow trees to tolerate a broader range of environmental conditions?**

Australia is an ideal place to ask these questions. Its forests contain an unusually high proportion of the world’s dual-mycorrhizal, EcM, and non-mycorrhizal tree species, but relatively few AM tree species compared with the global flora (Figure 1). That makes the continent a natural experiment in how plant–fungi strategies shape environmental niches.

We analysed 2,334 native Australian forest tree species across 17,764 grid cells at 10 × 10 km resolution, spanning the wet tropical rainforests of the northeast to the relatively dry and nutrient-poor forests of the southwest.

We found that mycorrhizal strategy is not a botanical footnote. It is a major clue to where trees occur, how much environmental variation they tolerate, and how Australian forests came to be organised across the continent.

## Analytical approach

We combined species distribution modelling, spatial statistics, phylogenetic correction, and null modelling to separate ecological signal from spatial, geometric, and evolutionary structure.

- **MaxEnt species distribution models** → estimated where each species can occur and the range of environmental conditions they can tolerate

- **INLA-SPDE Bayesian spatial random fields** → tested how the prevalence of each mycorrhizal strategy changed across Australia while accounting for spatial structure in the data

- **Phylogenetic inference** → tested whether niche patterns remained after accounting for evolutionary relatedness among species

- **Mid-domain null modelling** → tested whether latitudinal niche patterns reflected ecological signal or the geometry of the Australian continent

All pipelines were version-controlled and reproducible end-to-end.

## Key findings

### Dual-mycorrhizal trees had the broadest environmental niches

Dual-mycorrhizal trees capable of forming both AM and EcM partnerships occupied the widest range of environmental conditions (Figure 2).

Their niche breadth was **29–45% greater** than that of AM, EcM, and non-mycorrhizal trees. This suggests that dual-mycorrhizal species may be able to hedge against environmental change by drawing on both AM and EcM traits.

In ecological terms, they can leverage the benefits of multiple belowground strategies.

![Figure 2](/images/projects/environmental-niches/figure_2.png)

**Figure 2.** Dual-mycorrhizal trees occupied the broadest environmental niches across Australian forests. (**a**) Raw species-level niche breadth for each mycorrhizal type. (**b**) Mean phylogenetically adjusted differences among mycorrhizal types. Results are shown for all forests, tropical forests, and temperate forests. AM and EcM trees showed a clear climatic-zone reversal, with EcM trees having broader niches in tropical forests, while AM trees had broader niches in temperate forests. This suggests that niche breadth may expand when trees occur outside their core climatic zones.

### Dual-mycorrhizal trees bridge AM- and EcM-dominated forest biomes

Across Australia, AM trees were concentrated in warm, wet tropical forests, while EcM trees were more prominent in cooler temperate forests.

Dual-mycorrhizal trees bridged these two worlds.

They extended into mid-latitudes, linking AM-dominated tropical forests with EcM-dominated temperate forests (Figure 3). This makes them geographic intermediaries across one of the major latitudinal gradients in Australian forests.

### In environmental space, dual-mycorrhizal trees tracked EcM trees

Geographically, dual-mycorrhizal trees acted as a bridge. Environmentally, they told a different story.

Rather than occupying a neat middle ground between AM and EcM trees, dual-mycorrhizal species showed strong overlap with EcM trees in environmental space (Figure 4).

This distinction matters. Dual-mycorrhizal trees may connect AM- and EcM-dominated forests geographically, but their environmental preferences are more EcM-like.

![Figure 4](/images/projects/environmental-niches/figure_4.png)

**Figure 4.** Dual-mycorrhizal trees were geographically intermediate but environmentally EcM-like. Niche overlap analyses showed that dual-mycorrhizal species shared more environmental space with EcM trees than with AM trees, suggesting that the two strategies occupy similar environmental conditions despite different geographic patterns. (**a**) Kernel density estimates show where species from each mycorrhizal type occur in environmental space. (**b**) Schoener's D quantifies niche overlap among mycorrhizal types, where 0 indicates no overlap and 1 indicates complete overlap.

### Australian trees did not follow the classic Rapoport pattern

In the Northern Hemisphere, tree species often show broader niches at higher latitudes. This pattern, known as the Rapoport effect, is usually linked to strong climatic filtering and repeated glaciation throughout the Quaternary.

Australian forest trees showed a different pattern.

Niche breadth peaked closer to the middle of the continent’s latitudinal domain rather than increasing steadily toward higher latitudes (Figure 5). This was broadly consistent with a **mid-domain effect**, where the shape and boundaries of the continent influence how species ranges stack up across latitude.

EcM and dual-mycorrhizal trees partly diverged from this expected mid-domain pattern at higher latitudes, suggesting that geometry alone does not explain the full story.

That matters because Australian tree niches may carry a distinct Southern Hemisphere signal — one shaped by continental geometry, long-term climatic stability, limited glaciation, and deep evolutionary persistence.

![Figure 5](/images/projects/environmental-niches/figure_5.png)

**Figure 5.** Australian forest trees did not follow the classic Northern Hemisphere Rapoport pattern. Instead of increasing steadily toward higher latitudes, environmental niche breadth peaked closer to the middle of Australia’s latitudinal domain. Mid-domain null models suggest that this pattern partly reflects the geometry of the continent. EcM and dual-mycorrhizal trees diverged from null expectations at higher latitudes, suggesting that Australia’s temperate flora may also carry the imprint of long-term climatic stability, limited glaciation, and deep evolutionary persistence.

## Why it matters

Mycorrhizal type is often treated as background information. This study shows it can be a first-order predictor of how trees occupy environmental space.

Our findings have practical consequences. If tree–fungi strategies influence environmental tolerance, they can help improve vulnerability assessments, restoration planning, and predictions of how forests may respond to environmental change.

In short, the hidden belowground fungal partnerships in forests help explain the visible patterns we see aboveground.
