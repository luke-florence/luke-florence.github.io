## Overview

Fungi are essential to life on land. They drive nutrient cycling, support plant growth, shape soil carbon dynamics, and influence ecosystem stability.

Yet they remain a major blind spot in conservation.

Most fungal species are hidden belowground, difficult to observe directly, and poorly represented in the biodiversity datasets used for mapping, modelling, and environmental decision-making. DNA metabarcoding has changed what we can detect, but raw metabarcoding data are not automatically ready for conservation use.

That was the problem this project tackled.

The Australian Microbiome Initiative had generated an extraordinary soil fungal dataset: more than 2,000 topsoil samples spanning Australia, Christmas Island, and Antarctica (Figure 1). But historical versions of the processed data contained known artefacts, including inflated diversity estimates, index-switching errors, and overly permissive taxonomic assignments. Once integrated into biodiversity platforms, these issues risked producing misleading fungal occurrence records.

**AusMycobiome** is a ground-up reanalysis of that dataset.

We rebuilt the soil fungal resource using conservative, best-practice standards in fungal metabarcoding, then released it as a curated, analysis-ready dataset for biodiversity, biogeography, and conservation research.

## Scale

- **2,104 unique samples**
- **8 ecoregions** — from the warm, wet tropical forests of far north Queensland to the cold, dry Antarctic tundra
- **31,926 fungal OTUs** representing approximately 132 million quality-filtered sequences
- **More than 120 environmental predictors** — including climate, soil physicochemistry, vegetation, habitat condition, and geography — pre-joined to every sample

## What we fixed

Historical versions of the dataset had propagated three major problems into downstream biodiversity records:

- **Inflated diversity estimates**, driven by poor-quality single-end ITS reads processed with routine pipelines
- **Index-switching artefacts**, which can create ghost detections of taxa in samples where they do not belong, including widespread ectomycorrhizal fungal records in Antarctica
- **Lenient taxonomic assignments**, which incorrectly linked native Australian fungi to Northern Hemisphere species names

We built a conservative, reproducible pipeline to address these issues.

Rather than relying on one-size-fits-all thresholds, we developed a taxonomically informed clustering approach that used lineage-specific information to assign fungal OTUs more carefully.

This mattered because fungal metabarcoding is not just a sequencing problem. It is an inference problem. Small decisions about filtering, clustering, and taxonomy can change which species appear to occur where.

To validate the pipeline, we benchmarked AusMycobiome against three published studies that had identified weaknesses in historical versions of the dataset. We also validated the resource by reproducing a continental species distribution modelling study for orchid mycorrhizal fungi.

## Open science

Everything is open, version-controlled, and designed for reuse:

- Full bioinformatics pipeline on GitHub: [github.com/LukeLikesDirt/AusMycobiome](https://github.com/LukeLikesDirt/AusMycobiome)
- Curated OTU matrices, reference sequences, taxonomy tables, and sample metadata on figshare: [doi.org/10.6084/m9.figshare.27938037](https://doi.org/10.6084/m9.figshare.27938037)
- Ready-to-use data products for researchers who want to move straight from download to analysis
- Open-access manuscript in *Scientific Data*: [Florence et al. 2025](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41597-025-04598-5)

We advocate replacing historical Australian Microbiome fungal records on the Atlas of Living Australia and GBIF with this curated version, so downstream biodiversity queries draw on robust data rather than known artefacts.

## Conservation impact

Fungi are a kingdom-scale blind spot in conservation.

For plants and animals, conservation scientists routinely use range maps, species distribution models, vulnerability assessments, and restoration planning layers. For most fungi, those tools remain underdeveloped because the underlying occurrence data have not been fit for purpose.

AusMycobiome helps close that gap for Australia and Antarctica.

By linking robust fungal occurrence data with environmental predictors, the dataset can support biogeographic modelling, conservation prioritisation, and Southern Hemisphere ecological inference. It gives researchers a stronger foundation for asking where fungi occur, what environments they occupy, and how fungal communities may respond to environmental change.
